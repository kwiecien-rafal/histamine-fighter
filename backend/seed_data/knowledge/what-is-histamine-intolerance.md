## Overview

Histamine intolerance is not a true allergy. It describes a mismatch between the
amount of histamine entering or being released in the body and the body's ability
to break it down. When intake outpaces breakdown, histamine builds up and can
trigger symptoms. People who tolerate histamine well do not react the same way to
the same foods.

Histamine is a normal signalling molecule involved in immune responses, stomach
acid production, and nerve signalling. It is also present in many foods, and is
produced by bacteria as food ages or ferments. The problem in histamine
intolerance is reduced breakdown capacity, not histamine itself.

## Common symptoms

Symptoms vary widely between individuals and can affect several systems at once.
Frequently reported ones include headaches or migraine, flushing of the skin,
hives or itching, nasal congestion and sneezing, digestive complaints such as
bloating, cramping, or diarrhoea, and a racing heart. Because these overlap with
many other conditions, histamine intolerance is a diagnosis of exclusion: other
causes should be ruled out first.

## Why breakdown capacity matters

The body clears dietary histamine mainly with the enzyme diamine oxidase (DAO) in
the gut wall. When DAO activity is low — through gut conditions, certain
medications, or other factors — even a normal histamine load can produce symptoms.
This is why the same meal affects people differently, and why freshness and
portion size matter so much.

## Important note

This article is educational and is not medical advice. Histamine intolerance
should be assessed by a qualified clinician, ideally alongside a registered
dietitian, before starting any restrictive diet.
