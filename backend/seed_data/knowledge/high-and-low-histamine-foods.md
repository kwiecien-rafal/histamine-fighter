## Why freshness drives histamine

Histamine is not usually present in large amounts in fresh food; it accumulates as
food ages, ferments, or is stored, because bacteria convert the amino acid
histidine into histamine over time. This is why the single most useful rule is
"fresh is safer": the same fish can be low in histamine when very fresh and high
after a day in the fridge.

## Foods often higher in histamine

The foods most often poorly tolerated are aged or fermented. These include aged
cheeses, cured and fermented meats such as salami and prosciutto, fermented
vegetables like sauerkraut, soy products such as soy sauce and miso, vinegar and
pickled foods, and most alcohol, especially wine and beer. Fish that has not been
kept very cold is a classic trigger, since histamine forms quickly in fish flesh.

Some foods are described as histamine liberators or as being high in other
biogenic amines rather than histamine itself; tolerance to these is especially
individual and best confirmed by careful reintroduction.

## Foods often better tolerated

Freshly cooked meat and poultry, very fresh or frozen-at-sea fish, most fresh
vegetables, many fresh fruits, rice and most plain grains, and fresh dairy such as
ricotta or mozzarella eaten fresh are generally tolerated better. Cooking from
fresh ingredients and freezing leftovers promptly keeps the histamine load down.

## Important note

Food lists differ between sources and individual tolerance varies a great deal.
Treat any list as a starting point for guided testing, not a fixed rule, and work
with a dietitian where possible. This article is educational, not medical advice.
