## A test, not a forever diet

A low-histamine diet is usually used as a short diagnostic tool, not a permanent
way of eating. The common approach has three phases: a few weeks of reducing
high-histamine foods, then a structured reintroduction of foods one at a time to
find personal triggers, and finally a long-term diet that is as varied as each
person's tolerance allows. Staying broadly restricted for months without guidance
risks unnecessary nutritional gaps.

## Practical habits that help

Beyond which foods to choose, handling matters. Buying and cooking fresh, eating
leftovers quickly or freezing them straight away, and avoiding food that has sat
warm all reduce the histamine that forms during storage. Keeping a simple food and
symptom diary makes the reintroduction phase far more reliable, because symptom
timing is what links a food to a reaction.

## Working with a professional

Because symptoms overlap with many conditions and tolerance is so individual, the
reintroduction phase is best done with a registered dietitian. They can keep the
diet nutritionally adequate, help interpret the diary, and avoid the trap of
ruling out more and more foods on weak evidence.

## Important note

This article is educational and is not medical advice. Do not start a restrictive
diet for a child, during pregnancy, or alongside other medical conditions without
professional supervision.
